Vovinam Viet Vo Dao: The Essence of My Practice

Vovinam is not merely a sport or a fighting method; it is a comprehensive system of education that harmonizes body and mind. Below is an in-depth exploration of the art that has shaped my character for over 11 years.

1. Origins & History

Founded in 1938 by Grand Master Nguyen Loc, Vovinam was born in a context of struggle for independence, aiming to build a strong generation of Vietnamese youth.

The Founder's Vision: Grand Master Nguyen Loc synthesized traditional Vietnamese martial arts with elements of foreign systems (like Judo and Kung Fu) to create a modern, scientific, and effective method suited for the Vietnamese physique. His goal was to train the youth to free the country from colonial rule, not just through fighting, but through moral strength.

Expansion: After the events of 1954, the movement migrated to South Vietnam. Under the second Patriarch, Master Le Sang, the art was structured, codified, and popularized. Today, Vovinam is practiced in over 70 countries, recognized for its spectacular techniques and educational value.

The Theory of Cương Nhu Phối Triển: Unlike some arts that rely solely on brute strength (Hard) or evasion (Soft), Vovinam uses the interaction of both. It teaches to be "Hard" (unyielding) on principles but "Soft" (adaptable) in behavior.

The Salute: The hand placed on the heart symbolizes "Steel Hand, Benevolent Heart." It means that martial force should only be used with kindness and justice.

The Colors:
• Blue (Hope): Represents the ocean and the beginning of the journey.
• Yellow (Skin/Earth): Represents the martial art entering the skin/body (Instructor level).
• Red (Blood): Represents the art entering the blood (Master level).

2. Philosophy: Harmony

The core philosophy rests on "Cương Nhu Phối Triển": the harmony between Hard and Soft. It teaches flexibility in adaptation and strength in conviction.

3. Technical Richness

Vovinam is famous for its diversity: punches, kicks, wrestling (Vat), weapons, and its signature technique: the Flying Scissors (Don Chan).

Đòn Chân Tấn Công (Flying Scissors): This is the trademark of Vovinam. Historically developed to unhorse colonial cavalrymen, these 21 techniques involve gripping the opponent's neck, waist, or legs with one's own legs to take them down. Achieving these requires immense agility and core strength.

Forms (Quyền) & Weapons: The curriculum includes codified forms (Quyen) performed bare-handed or with weapons such as the Long Staff (Côn), Sword (Kiếm), Machete (Mã Tấu), and Halberd (Đại Đao). As a 2nd Dan, I have mastered advanced forms utilizing these traditional weapons.

Song Luyen: These are dual training drills performed with a partner at high speed. They develop timing, distance management, and trust between partners.

Resilience & Problem Solving: In combat, as in engineering, things rarely go exactly as planned. Vovinam has taught me to remain calm under pressure, analyze the situation rapidly (the opponent's opening / a technical bug), and apply an effective solution.

Pedagogy & Leadership: preparing for my DIF (Instructor Diploma) requires me to deconstruct complex movements into learnable steps for students. This mirrors the engineering requirement to explain technical concepts to non-technical stakeholders.

Long-Term Vision: Reaching the 2nd Dan took 11 years of consistent effort. This proves my ability to commit to long-term goals and maintain high standards of quality over time.

4. Why It Matters

Beyond the dojo, Vovinam is a school of life. It has forged my resilience, my leadership skills, and my ability to analyze complex situations under pressure.

"Being strong to be useful." (Khỏe để phụng sự).
This motto drives my daily life. I strive to build my physical and intellectual strength not for personal glory, but to contribute effectively to my team and my future projects.
